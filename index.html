AI Exposure by Pay Band

Higher-paid workers face more AI exposure — because expensive knowledge work (analysis, writing, coding) is exactly what AI does best. Anthropic's research confirms: highly exposed workers earn 47% more than unexposed ones. This is the opposite of past automation waves.

The Premium: 56% Wage Gap, 3x Industry Divide

PwC's analysis of ~1B job postings: AI-skilled roles pay 56% more — a premium that more than doubled in roughly a year (25% in their 2024 report to 56% in their 2025 report). AI-exposed industries see 27% productivity growth vs 9% for unexposed — a 3x gap. Meanwhile, AI-requiring job postings grew +7.5% even as total postings fell -11.3%.

The AI Wage Premium Is Accelerating

PwC's analysis of ~1B job postings shows the wage premium for AI-skilled roles more than doubled in roughly a year — from 25% in their 2024 report to 56% in their 2025 report. The market signal is clear: AI skills are the most valuable differentiator in hiring.

Industry Productivity Gap

PwC found AI-exposed industries achieved 27% productivity growth (2018–2024) compared to just 9% for unexposed industries — a 3x gap that explains why the wage premium exists.

Measured Productivity Gains from AI (6 Studies)

These aren't projections — they're measured results from controlled experiments. The range is remarkably consistent: 15–56% improvement. Notably, Brynjolfsson et al. found the largest gains for the lowest-performing workers (+36%), suggesting AI compresses the skill gap rather than widening it.

Theoretical AI Capability vs Actual Usage by Sector

The purple bars show what percentage of tasks AI could theoretically handle in each sector (Eloundou et al. β metric). The blue bars show actual AI usage today (Anthropic, ~2M Claude conversations). The gap between them is why the premium exists — workers who close it capture outsized value.

The Equalizer: Education Matters Less Than AI Skill

The data shows a clear trend: degree requirements are dropping 7–9 percentage points for AI-augmented roles (PwC), and the biggest productivity gains go to the lowest performers — 36% for the bottom quintile vs 15% average (Brynjolfsson et al., n=5,172). AI proficiency is increasingly competing with credentials as a signal of capability.

The Window: 90% of Your Competitors Haven't Started

Only ~10% of US firms use AI in production (Census Bureau BTOS, Sept 2025). Even in the Information sector — the most AI-forward — over 60% of firms haven't started. The disruption we're seeing is from early adopters only. There's still time to get ahead.

AI Adoption Is Still Early (Census Bureau)

Census Bureau Business Trends and Outlook Survey data. AI adoption roughly doubled from 4.6% to ~10% in under a year — but 90% of firms still haven't started. The competitive window is open, but closing fast as adoption follows an S-curve.

What You Can Do Now

  • Start using AI in your actual work this week. The productivity data is clear: gains come from applying AI to real tasks — drafting, research, data cleanup, code review — not from taking a course about AI.
  • Focus on judgment, not output. AI generates drafts, code, and analysis faster than you can. The premium is shifting to people who can evaluate, refine, and direct AI output — knowing what to ask and when the answer is wrong.
  • Treat the coverage gap as your window. Only ~10% of firms use AI in production. Most of your competitors haven't started. Every month you spend building AI fluency now compounds into advantage as adoption accelerates.

Methodology & Data Accuracy

Every number on this page is sourced from published research, federal data, or peer-reviewed experiments. No statistics are invented. Below is a full accounting of each data source, what it covers, and its limitations.

Occupation Data (BLS)

All 342 occupations from the Bureau of Labor Statistics Occupational Outlook Handbook — the federal government's authoritative source on US employment. Includes median pay, employment counts, education requirements, and 10-year growth projections. BLS explicitly factors AI into projections: they modeled AI impact on specific occupations like medical transcriptionists (-4.7%), customer service reps (-5.0%), paralegals (1.2%), and software developers (+17.9%).

Federal government data · BLS AI Methodology (Feb 2025)

AI Exposure Scoring (JoshKale/jobs)

Each occupation's AI exposure score (0–10) was generated by sending the full BLS Markdown description to Gemini Flash (via OpenRouter) with a calibrated rubric. The rubric anchors: roofers/landscapers at 0–1, nurses/police at 4–5, developers/analysts at 8–9, data entry at 10. Key signal: is the work fundamentally digital? Scores are cross-validated against Anthropic's independent β metric.

Anthropic Economic Index

Published research introducing "observed exposure" — combining theoretical capability (β metric from Eloundou et al.) with real Claude usage data from millions of conversations. 57% of AI use augments work; 43% automates tasks. As of Jan 2025, 36% of sampled jobs see Claude used for 25%+ of tasks (rising to 49% across pooled reports).

Real usage data from Claude · Anthropic Economic Index

Empirical Productivity Studies

Six peer-reviewed experiments: Noy & Zhang (2023): 40% time reduction, +18% quality (n=453). Brynjolfsson, Li & Raymond (2023): 15% avg gain, 36% for bottom quintile (n=5,172). Peng et al.: GitHub Copilot 55.8% faster. Cui et al. (2025): 26% more weekly tasks (n=5,000). St. Louis Fed: 33% more productive during AI use. Key pattern: AI disproportionately benefits lower-performing workers.

PwC Global AI Jobs Barometer

Analysis of close to a billion job postings across 6 continents. AI-skilled roles pay 56% more (more than doubled in roughly a year). AI-exposed industries see 27% productivity growth vs 9% for unexposed (3x gap). AI-requiring postings grew +7.5% even as total postings fell -11.3%. Degree requirements dropping 7–9pp in AI roles. Skills in exposed roles changing 66% faster.

Brynjolfsson/ADP Employment Study

Brynjolfsson, Chandar & Chen (2025), Stanford Digital Economy Lab. Analyzed 3.5–5M workers monthly via ADP payroll data (25M+ US workers total). Found 16% relative employment decline for ages 22–25 in high-exposure occupations, but distinguishes automation (declining) from augmentation (growing). Updated November 2025.

3.5–5M workers/month · Stanford DEL

Counterpoint: EIG & Yale Budget Lab

EIG (2025) tested 5 AI exposure measures against CPS data and found no AI-driven job losses. Yale Budget Lab (2026) compared 7 measures and found "stability, not major disruption." Brookings (March 2026): "evidence is inconclusive" and warns of "narrator bias." These counterpoints are important context: the evidence is genuinely mixed.

AI Adoption Data (Census Bureau)

Census Bureau Business Trends and Outlook Survey (BTOS). Only ~10% of firms use AI in production (Sept 2025), up from 4.6% in early 2024. Even in the Information sector, >60% of firms don't use AI. This is critical context: most of the disruption is still ahead.

Federal survey data · Census BTOS

AI Improvement Rate & Limitations

Anthropic's coverage gap is closing — usage rose from 36% to 49% of sampled jobs between reports. We model an S-curve adoption where effects accelerate mid-decade. Key limitations: (1) AI advancement may be faster or slower than modeled; (2) policy/regulation could alter outcomes; (3) new job categories will emerge; (4) individual outcomes vary enormously. Projections are directional, not precise.
